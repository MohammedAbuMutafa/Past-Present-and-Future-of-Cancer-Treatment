Introduction

WHAT IS CANCER

The National Cancer Institute (NIH) defines cancer as a disease where a person's cells start to multiply uncontrollably and rapidly and begin to spread to other parts of the body (“What Is Cancer?,” 2021). Cells sometimes multiply quickly, form tumors, and spread to other body parts through tissues. Mostly cancer is a genetic disease which means that changes in the genes control cell processes. But in the present day, we can see that cancer can also be a side effect of an unhealthy lifestyle. The name cancer comes from “father of medicine” who found the differences between ulcer-forming tumors and non-ulcer-forming tumors (Understanding What Cancer Is: Ancient Times to Present, n.d.).

(Thirdman, n.d.)

CANCER RESEARCH START

When cancer was first discovered, the ancients believed that it cannot be cured after the tumor spreads around the body. In addition, operating surgery to remove the tumor was very complex back then because of the high levels of blood loss. After anesthesia was discovered Physicians were more optimistic about cancer treatment. In the 19th century, William Stewart Halsted, a professor from John Hopkins University, found a way to remove the cancer cells as well as the affected lymphatic nodes. This process is called radical surgery. This was the only progress cancer treatment had for four centuries. Radiation and other treatments were more recent inventions for cancer treatment (Understanding What Cancer Is: Ancient Times to Present, n.d.).

(Pixabay, n.d.)

CURRENT RESEARCH

Cancer treatment saw more improvements and discoveries including chemotherapy and radiation. As of 2020, 18,094,716 million cases of cancer were diagnosed with 40% survival of risk factors (WCRF International, 2022). Cancer research is still ongoing and we have come a long way in terms of treatment. Although there is no proper cure for cancer, doctors have found ways to make cancer a less sufferable disease and bring ease to its victims. Chemotherapy and radiation are the most common procedures of cancer treatments along with radical surgery. Chemotherapy treats cancer by using drugs to kill the cancer cells in the body. Moreover, radiation uses x-rays to kill fast-growing cells that form tumors (“What Is Cancer?,” 2021). The evolution of cancer treatment has increased the number of cancer survivors and cleared the way for future advancements. A future improvement can be the establishment of immunomodulation, which treats cancer with the use of checkpoint blockade inhibitors on the immune system. Immunomodulation, with more research, has great potential to be a cure for cancer.
